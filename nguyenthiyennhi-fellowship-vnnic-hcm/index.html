During my studies in Information Technology, I began to view the Internet not only as a user-facing platform, but as an infrastructure system with clear scalability constraints. As I explored system design and architecture in more depth, I realized that:

03IPv4 Exhaustion

IPv4 – The Scalability Limit of the Modern Internet

IPv4 was designed in the early 1980s for an Internet with relatively limited connectivity demands. Today, the Internet has evolved into the operational backbone of the global digital economy, where AI systems, cloud computing, IoT ecosystems, and billions of connected devices continuously push network architecture beyond its original assumptions.

The challenge is no longer simply about address availability. It is about sustaining modern digital infrastructure on an architecture approaching its scalability limits.

No remaining free IPv4 pools

  • Major Internet registries such as APNIC, RIPE NCC, and ARIN have largely exhausted publicly available IPv4 allocations
  • IPv4 distribution increasingly depends on reuse, recovery, and address transfer markets

NAT: a temporary workaround becoming default architecture

  • NAT extends IPv4 lifespan but breaks the Internet's original end-to-end connectivity model
  • Services such as VoIP, peer-to-peer systems, gaming, and IoT increasingly operate under architectural constraints introduced by IPv4 limitations

Rising infrastructure costs

  • IPv4 leasing and transfer costs continue to increase as global scarcity deepens
  • Technology startups, cloud-native enterprises, and IoT deployments increasingly absorb the hidden cost of legacy Internet architecture

04Why IPv6

IPv6 is not a minor upgrade. It is a re-foundation of Internet architecture.

340 undecillion addresses (3.4 × 10³⁸) — enough to assign an address to virtually every device, sensor, system, and endpoint in the foreseeable future. This is not a capacity extension; it is a rebuilding of architecture for global-scale Internet.

IPv6 doesn't just give you 'more IPs'. It removes the layers of workarounds IPv4 forced us to accumulate over decades — especially NAT, which has made the Internet increasingly complex, difficult to scale, and hard to manage.

IPv6 was designed to:

  • Enable direct end-to-end connectivity
  • Support automatic device configuration
  • Scale to billions of new systems
  • Better support cloud, mobile, and IoT
  • Reduce routing and network operations complexity

06IPv6 × Artificial Intelligence

AI doesn’t scale on GPUs alone. It scales on networks that can connect and coordinate at massive scale.

Every modern AI model — from distributed training and inference clusters to edge AI — communicates through IP addresses. When a hyperscale AI system spans tens of thousands of GPUs across multiple datacenters, the challenge is no longer just compute. It is:

  • identity
  • routing
  • synchronization
  • data transfer at global scale

IPv6 becomes the mandatory foundation layer for modern AI architecture.

Distributed AI needs end-to-end addressing

  • One hyperscale training run spans multiple GPU clusters, cloud regions, and datacenters globally
  • Direct per-node identity — no NAT, no overlay complexity
  • Near-unlimited address space for elastic AI fabric expansion

Edge AI & On-device Inference

  • AI’s future lives on phones, autonomous vehicles, robots, smart cameras, IoT edge devices
  • IPv6 provides globally routable addresses for billions of concurrent AI endpoints

Federated Learning requires ultra-low latency

  • Federated models need fast synchronization, stable connections, real-time gradient transfer
  • IPv4 + NAT adds address translation layers and network complexity
  • IPv6 removes bottlenecks and optimizes end-to-end communication between AI nodes

AI-aware Traffic Engineering

  • IPv6 + Segment Routing (SRv6) + real-time telemetry + dynamic traffic engineering
  • Networks self-optimize data flows by AI workload type
  • Prioritize inference or training traffic in real time

09IPv6 × 5G Future

5G is not just faster wireless technology. It is the connectivity architecture for an ultra-dense digital society.

5G handles the radio access layer. IPv6 handles identity, routing, and scaling to millions of devices. In other words:

5G is the wireless connectivity layer. IPv6 is the data circulatory system beneath it.

Why 5G was designed around IPv6

Massive Machine-Type Communications (mMTC)

10⁶ devices/km²

  • 5G supports urban sensors, connected vehicles, industrial IoT, and millions of simultaneous endpoints
  • That device density cannot operate on IPv4’s limited address space

URLLC requires direct connectivity

  • Autonomous vehicles, industrial robots, remote surgery, and real-time control infrastructure demand ultra-low latency
  • Deep NAT in IPv4 adds complexity and latency — incompatible with URLLC requirements
  • IPv6 enables true end-to-end connectivity — exactly what 5G was designed to leverage

5G Core is a cloud-native architecture

  • 5G Core operates on Service-Based Architecture (SBA), microservices, service mesh, HTTP/2-native
  • This entire architecture aligns naturally with IPv6-native networks at scale

Network Slicing & intelligent QoS

  • 5G creates multiple network slices for healthcare, industry, IoT, gaming, and urban infrastructure
  • IPv6 provides better support for traffic engineering, flow labeling, QoS, and segment routing
  • Enables real-time per-service resource allocation

10IPv6 × Cybersecurity

IPv6 does not automatically make networks more secure. It removes the architectural constraints that make modern security hard to implement correctly.

For years, the Internet had to patch IPv4’s problems with NAT, intermediary layers, bolt-on encryption, and address-hiding architectures instead of true identity management. IPv6 changes that foundation.

IPv6 enables a modern security model

IPSec integrated from the foundation

  • In IPv4, IPSec is a bolt-on component
  • In IPv6, IPSec is integrated as part of the protocol architecture
  • Provides better support for end-to-end encryption, node authentication, and large-scale secure connectivity

Zero-Trust needs real identity capability

  • Modern zero-trust models require per-endpoint identification, continuous authentication, and identity-based control
  • IPv6 makes this more viable with end-to-end global addresses, reduced NAT dependency, and clearer endpoint governance

Cryptographically Generated Addresses (CGA)

  • IPv6 supports CGA mechanisms that bind identity to addresses
  • Increases node trustworthiness, reduces spoofing risk in certain network models

Simpler header, better inspection capability

  • IPv6 header design is more compact and consistent, reducing packet-processing ambiguity
  • Helps firewalls, IDS/IPS, telemetry engines, and traffic analysis systems operate more effectively at scale

But IPv6 also requires higher operational maturity

  • Misconfiguration can expand the attack surface — IPv6 is not turn on and secure
  • Monitoring systems may not fully see IPv6 traffic if not updated
  • Successful transition requires engineer training, SOC process updates, monitoring upgrades, and new security thinking

The modern Internet cannot rely indefinitely on patches built for 1980s networking. IPv6 is not a complete security solution — but it is the foundation on which modern security architecture can stand.

“

A modern cybersecurity posture needs a protocol designed for the modern era.

11Reality Check

Vietnam is one of the most successful IPv6 deployment stories in the region. But a mature strategy looks directly at the gaps that remain.

Those gaps are not failures. They are signs of an ecosystem transitioning at national scale.

01

Specialized workforce gap

  • IPv6 capability concentrated at major ISPs, national infrastructure agencies, and dedicated technical teams
  • SMEs, local enterprises, and provincial infrastructure still lack engineers capable of IPv6-native design, dual-stack operations, and next-generation security deployment
IPv6 workforce45%
02

Legacy systems still IPv4-dependent

  • Many enterprise applications, government systems, legacy network equipment, and SME infrastructure are still built around IPv4
  • This creates transition costs, operational complexity, and a psychology of upgrade deferral
03

Awareness asymmetry

  • Large corporations view IPv6 as long-term strategy, cloud & AI infrastructure, and future scaling capability
  • Many SMEs still view IPv6 as a technical cost rather than a competitive capability — this gap is primarily a perception gap
Market awareness40%
04

Geographic unevenness

  • IPv6 deployment strongest in major urban centers, data centers, and national backbone networks
  • Local networks, smaller ISPs, and rural areas are still transitioning more slowly
Deployment coverage60%

A country strong in digital infrastructure is not one without problems. It is one that identifies problems accurately, acknowledges gaps openly, and builds capability to close them.

13National Roadmap to 2030

2030 is not a 'technical deadline'. It is the strategic horizon Vietnam is navigating toward.

2030 is not an administrative milestone. It is the transition threshold — when IPv6 stops being an option and becomes a baseline requirement. The three phases below are not a linear plan — they are three layers necessary for the transition to happen at national scale: awareness, deployment, and sovereignty.

01 2026 – 2027

Foundation

Awareness · Education · Workforce

Strategic priorities

  • Integrate IPv6 into university and technical curricula
  • Standardize training content for ISPs and enterprises
  • Launch workforce certification programs for technical engineers
  • Build a national IPv6 knowledge and resource base
  • Run coordinated awareness campaigns via VNNIC and Ministry of IT

Expected outcomes

  • 50% of technical universities offer IPv6 programs
  • 10,000 certified network engineers
  • Public awareness reaches critical threshold
02 2027 – 2028

Expansion

SMEs · Cloud · Local Infrastructure

Strategic priorities

  • Nationwide IPv6 transition support program for SMEs
  • Mandate all government cloud platforms to be IPv6-native
  • Province-by-province network upgrade with prioritized rollout waves
  • Expand IPv6 testing toolkits and validation frameworks
  • Embed IPv6 criteria into public procurement policy

Expected outcomes

  • 60% of SMEs complete IPv6 transition
  • Government cloud infrastructure reaches 80% IPv6
03 2028 – 2030

IPv6-First Vietnam

Full stack · Smart Cities · Next-generation infrastructure

Strategic priorities

  • Migrate all public digital services to IPv6-only
  • Synchronize Smart City ecosystems with IPv6 infrastructure
  • Target 90–99% national IPv6 deployment rate
  • Position Vietnam as the SEA model for IPv6 transition
  • Export policy frameworks and knowledge to ASEAN partners

Expected outcomes

  • 90–99% national IPv6 adoption achieved
  • Regional leadership in digital sovereignty
  • Reference model for developing nations worldwide

These three phases do not happen in isolation. They overlap, reinforce each other, and require parallel resourcing. Phase 1 builds the workforce Phase 2 needs. Phase 2 builds the infrastructure Phase 3 depends on.

2030 is not the end of the transition. It is the moment the next-generation Internet begins to become the default.
