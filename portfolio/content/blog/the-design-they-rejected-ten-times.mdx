The tenth round of feedback arrived close to midnight. I did not need to open it to guess the shape of the message, because the nine before it had all sounded almost the same: "Could you take another look? It is still not quite right."

I sat there staring at the Figma file glowing on my screen. This was the design I had lost sleep over, the one whose margins I had polished and whose colors I secretly thought were beautiful. For the first time, I hated looking at it.

More honestly, I hated what it seemed to say about me.

---

## Every revision felt like a verdict

There is a particular sting in sending out something you made carefully and receiving only a "not yet." It does not feel like feedback at first. It feels like someone looking at the part you are proudest of and shaking their head.

Round one, I nodded and fixed it. Round two, I felt a small twinge. By rounds three and four, my face started to burn when the notification came in. By the seventh, I was sitting in my room with my eyes stinging, asking the question people rarely say out loud: *what if I simply do not have the talent?*

I opened old versions and compared them with the newest one. To me, the first one still looked better. That made the feedback feel even more unfair.

The voice in my head was not generous. She is picky. She does not understand taste. She is ruining the design. I carried that resentment into bed, then woke up with it still sitting in my chest.

By the tenth round, I had already drafted a polite withdrawal message in my head, mostly to protect my pride.

---

## The eleventh round

I am not sure why I did not quit that night. Maybe I was too tired to keep defending myself.

I poured a glass of water, sat down, and did something I had not honestly done in the first ten rounds: I reread every comment slowly, without preparing my argument while reading.

That is when I noticed the thing I had been missing.

She had never once called me bad. She kept talking about the **users**. "These folks are older, so the text needs to be bigger." "They use their phones outside, so this background will be hard to read." "They are in a hurry, so do not make them tap through so many steps."

Across ten rounds, what had I been defending? My taste. The layout I found elegant. The palette I found artistic. The version of the work that made me feel talented.

I was designing for myself to admire, then getting angry that the real user did not applaud.

> I thought they were rejecting my work. Turns out they were just trying to show me a person I'd forgotten existed.

On the eleventh round, I stopped trying to prove I had good taste. I pictured the nurse in a hurry, hands damp, eyes tired, trying to finish one task before the next patient arrived. Then I redesigned for her.

I sent it off without holding my breath for praise. Two hours later, the reply came back: "That works. Good job."

The feeling was not victory. It was a little shame, and a lot of relief.

<KeyTakeaway label="Something I noted down" tone="terracotta">
Separating your ego from the thing you make is difficult because the work carries your effort. But critique is not always a verdict on you. Often it is a map to a person or problem you have not looked at closely enough.
</KeyTakeaway>

---

I still get work sent back. That is part of making anything for other people. But one thing changed for good: I stopped reading feedback as a verdict first.

Now, when someone says "not yet," I try to ask a better first question: *what are they seeing that I am not?* That question does not mean I accept every comment blindly. It just moves me out of the defensive crouch and back beside the person I am making the thing for.

I think a lot of life works this way. Not just design. Sometimes we argue with someone we love and defend our logic so fiercely that we forget to ask where they are hurting. Sometimes we protect our version of the story so tightly that no new information can get in.

The next time someone says something hard to hear, you do not have to nod along immediately. Just try one pass without your guard fully raised. Ask what they might be protecting, noticing, or needing.

You may still disagree. But at least you will be responding to the real signal, not only to the bruise on your pride.
