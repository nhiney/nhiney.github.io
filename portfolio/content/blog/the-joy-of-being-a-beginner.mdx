Yesterday I spent almost an hour making four tiny icons.

Any experienced designer could probably finish them in fifteen minutes. Mine took a full evening of nudging lines, changing colors, undoing, redoing, opening tutorials, and asking questions so basic they made me laugh at myself.

Why does this corner look sharp when the border-radius says it should be round?

Is this font actually clean, or have I just stared at it too long?

Why does moving something two pixels make the whole thing suddenly feel calmer?

When the last icon finally sat in place, I leaned back and had to stop myself from cheering out loud. To a professional, the work was probably "not bad." To me, it felt like discovering electricity.

That kind of joy belongs to beginners. I want to keep it as long as I can.

---

## The pressure to be good immediately

There is a quiet pressure around learning now: if you start something, you are expected to become competent almost instantly.

I have felt it many times. I open a design tool for the first time and somehow end up comparing myself to polished portfolios on Behance. I am halfway through a tutorial and already thinking about people my age who have built full products, won competitions, or made work that looks impossibly mature.

That pressure makes learning strangely joyless. Instead of exploring, I start performing. Instead of asking, "what happens if I try this?", I ask, "does this make me look like I know what I am doing?"

But the more I learn, the more I think the beginning of a skill is not a defective version of expertise. It is its own beautiful stage.

When you know very little, everything is still alive. You have fewer rules in your head, fewer habits to protect, fewer assumptions about what a "proper" result should look like. An accidental color combination can surprise you. A layout mistake can teach you composition. A bad first attempt can reveal the question you actually need to ask.

This is what people call the **beginner's mind**. Suzuki Roshi wrote that in the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, while in the expert's mind there are few. I understand that line more every time I learn something new.

Experts often have to work hard to see freshly again. Beginners get that freshness for free.

---

## Going slowly is not the same as falling behind

I am not naturally fast at learning.

Some people watch a tutorial once and can repeat the technique immediately. I usually need to pause, rewind, open three extra tabs, try it wrong, try it worse, and then slowly understand why it works.

I used to treat that as evidence against myself. Now I think slowness has a hidden advantage: it makes me notice details I would miss if I were racing.

I notice that a button feels more expensive when the padding breathes. I notice that one grey feels warmer than another because of a tiny shift in undertone. I notice that an icon becomes friendlier when the stroke is rounded, and stricter when it is sharp. These are small things, but design is made of small things.

Going slowly gives me time to develop taste instead of only collecting techniques.

The goal is not to be slow forever. The goal is to stop treating slow learning as shameful. Understanding something deeply often requires a period where you look confused, ask obvious questions, and produce work that does not yet match your imagination.

That gap can feel humiliating. It is also where the real learning happens.

---

## Ask the simple question

If you are starting anything right now — UX/UI, code, photography, writing, ceramics, a new language — and you feel painfully behind, try to protect your beginner stage instead of apologizing for it.

Ask the question that feels too simple.

"Why does this spacing feel wrong?"

"What is the difference between padding and margin in the way a screen feels?"

"Why does this sentence sound flat even though the grammar is correct?"

Those are not silly questions. They are signs that you are paying attention.

One day the basics will become obvious to you. You may even forget how strange they once felt. When that day comes, you will have to work to recover the curiosity you naturally have now.

So do not rush through the beginning just to look impressive sooner.

Let yourself be clumsy. Let yourself be excited by tiny progress. Let yourself enjoy the rare privilege of not knowing yet.

You are not behind the journey. You are at the part where the world is still opening.
