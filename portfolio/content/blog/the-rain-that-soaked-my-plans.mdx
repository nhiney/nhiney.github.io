I was maybe two hundred meters from the innovation hub when the sky opened.

Not a polite drizzle. The kind of rain that arrives all at once, loud and heavy, as if someone tipped a bucket over the city. I ran with both arms around my laptop bag, pressing it to my chest like something alive. Those two hundred meters felt much longer than they had any right to be.

By the time I reached the lobby, I was soaked from my hairline to my shoes. Water gathered under me in a small, embarrassing puddle.

I had prepared for that session for almost a week. Slides adjusted until two in the morning. Demo tested again and again. Outfit ironed flat, now stuck to my skin. I pulled out the laptop, pressed the power button, and watched nothing happen.

Black screen. Another press. Still black. A strange line of text flashed once, then vanished.

---

## Two hundred meters and a small panic

I sank into a lobby chair, cold and dripping, while my brain tried to bargain with reality.

Borrow someone's machine? The files were on mine. Present without slides? I had rehearsed with them so many times that my memory felt attached to each click. Ask to go later? Maybe. Run home? Impossible.

I almost cried in that very specific way where anger and self-pity become the same thing. A whole week of effort, undone by weather.

Then, strangely, I laughed. Not because it was fine. It was not fine. I laughed because the situation had become almost too honest. All week I had worried about stumbling over my words, getting a hard question, and whether slide seven looked weak. I had worried about everything except the sky, which turned out to be the one thing strong enough to stop the plan.

---

## Sitting and watching it fall

When there is nothing left to force, the body eventually stops fighting. I sat there for a while and watched rainwater run down the glass in long streaks.

While I watched it, I understood something I had known intellectually for years: there is a clean line between the part I can hold and the part I cannot.

I can hold preparation. I can hold effort. I can hold how I speak to people when plans fail. I cannot hold the sky. I cannot hold a wet laptop. I cannot hold whether the day will cooperate with the version I wrote in my head.

> I poured a whole week into making the part I control flawless. Then a cloud — the thing I couldn't control at all — erased it in five minutes.

My exhaustion that day did not come only from the rain. It came from insisting that, because I had prepared well, reality owed me a clean path.

<KeyTakeaway label="Something I keep" tone="sage">
You are responsible for your part: preparation, effort, honesty, kindness. The rest belongs to weather, timing, systems, and other people. Peace begins when you stop treating the uncontrollable part as a personal failure.
</KeyTakeaway>

The security guard noticed me shivering and handed me a dry towel. I wiped the laptop down, placed it near a fan in the lobby, and asked the organizer if I could present later if the machine came back. That was the part I could still do.

Half an hour later, when the rain had softened, the laptop flickered back to life as if it had not just rearranged my entire nervous system. I missed the beginning of the session. They let me present afterward anyway.

The part I had feared most — fumbling — went more smoothly than expected, probably because I had no energy left to perform perfection. I just explained the project plainly.

---

Now, when a plan breaks, I think back to that lobby. Not as a cute story about resilience, but as a practical image: me, soaked, finally separating the next useful action from the drama around it.

Some rains cannot be dodged, no matter how carefully you check the forecast. But even in the rain, there is usually one small thing still available: ask for help, dry the laptop, move the schedule, tell the truth, begin again.

That is enough responsibility for one person to carry.
