I still remember the silence right after I shouted the last sentence.

It isn't the anger I remember most. It's the stillness that came after it — the room suddenly thick, the person across from me looking at me with an expression I'd never seen before, and me standing there, hands still shaking, realizing I'd just burned something I had no way to get back.

I'm telling you this from the position of someone who has held the match. Not from above, looking down.

---

## Three seconds to light

What I came to see, after many times, is this: anger is dangerously cheap.

It demands almost no thought from me. It requires no courage. It needs only a spark — a sentence that grazes my pride, a look I read as contempt, a small inconvenience arriving on the wrong day — and within three seconds my ego flares into flame.

And fire always gives me a false sensation. It makes me feel strong, right, and in control. The body plays along too. The heart races, blood rushes up, adrenaline roars through me, and for a moment I mistake intensity for power.

But anger is not strength. It is an energy thief wearing strength as a costume.

It borrows from me. Borrows fast, lets me spend lavishly for a few minutes, then leaves a bill I have to pay over many days, sometimes many years. While I'm burning, I feel rich. Only when the fire dies do I see what I actually spent.

---

## Putting out the fire is the easy part

Here's what no one told me when I was young: stopping the anger isn't the end. It's the moment the heaviest work begins.

When the flame is out, I kneel down in the ashes and see what it left. A sentence I let slip, now carved into the other person's memory. A bit of trust burned away — the kind that takes years to build and can disappear in one line. And strangest, most painful of all, a patch of my own self-respect turned to ash too. I look back at who I was mid-rage, and I don't recognize him.

> Lighting the fire costs three seconds. Clearing the ash costs months. And some things, no matter how carefully you clean, leave a smoke stain that never fully comes off.

Why is cleaning the wreckage so much harder than preventing it? Because while I'm cleaning, the adrenaline is gone. I'm sober. I have to face the consequences with a cold head and a heavy heart. I have to apologize, and a real apology hurts far more than the fight did. I have to rebuild trust one brick at a time, while the other person has every right to doubt me.

Preventing a fire takes one moment of restraint. Cleaning one up takes a whole process — steady, quiet, with no thrill in it at all.

<KeyTakeaway label="What I learned" tone="terracotta">
The question is not "am I allowed to be angry?" Of course you are. The better question is: if I turn this anger into a weapon, who will have to repair the damage later?
</KeyTakeaway>

---

## A pause before the match

I won't tell you to "calm down." I hate that line, because it is useless at the exact moment you need it most, and it is usually said by people who don't have to clean up your ashes.

What helps me more is a smaller pause. Not a saintly pause. Not a perfect pause. Just enough space to ask: *what am I about to protect, and what am I about to destroy?*

Sometimes anger is a signal that a boundary has been crossed. Sometimes it points to something real: exhaustion, disrespect, fear, hurt. I don't want to pretend the signal is meaningless. But a signal is not the same as a command. I can listen to anger without handing it the keys to my mouth.

So now, when I feel that familiar spark catch in my chest, I try not to crush it with willpower. I ask one plain question, by habit: *this fire — who's going to be the one cleaning it up later?*

Usually, that question is enough to make me set the match back down.
