I was cleaning out my drive when I found the first design file I ever made.

I opened it and laughed almost immediately.

The colors fought each other. The text had no breathing room. Buttons sat at odd sizes, like they had each been designed on a different planet. It was not "rough but promising" in the generous way people say when they are being kind. It was simply bad.

Then, after the laugh, I felt something lighter.

That ugly file was not evidence that I should have stopped. It was evidence that I had started. It was the version that gave me enough distance to learn taste, enough material to revise, enough practice to one day recognize what was wrong.

---

## Perfectionism is often fear in formal clothes

There are projects I have "started" many times without touching the actual work.

I researched tools. Saved references. Built plans. Compared frameworks. Rearranged the order of steps. Waited for a cleaner idea. Waited for the right mood. Waited until the project felt impressive enough in my head that making it real could only ruin it.

Preparation can be useful. But there is a point where preparation becomes a polite hiding place.

For me, the fear underneath was simple: the first version would show me my actual level.

A blank page lets me imagine that I am capable of something brilliant. A first draft makes the gap visible. The interface is clumsy. The paragraph is flat. The code is awkward. Suddenly the fantasy has a shape, and the shape disappoints me.

So I kept choosing the blank page and calling it high standards.

---

## You need something to edit

A terrible first draft does one generous thing: it moves the problem out of your head.

Once the thing exists, you can point to it. This button is too loud. This opening is slow. This section is confusing. This function is doing too much. The criticism becomes specific, and specific problems can be worked on.

An idea trapped in your head cannot receive feedback. It cannot be improved by taste, time, or revision. It can only become heavier.

> You cannot refine what does not exist. The messy first version is not the enemy of good work; it is the doorway into it.

That is why the first draft is allowed to be bad. Its job is not to prove that you are talented. Its job is to create a surface where learning can happen.

<KeyTakeaway label="A reminder to myself" tone="terracotta">
Make the rough screen. Write the flat paragraph. Build the clumsy prototype. Then improve one visible thing. Progress becomes possible when the work has somewhere to land.
</KeyTakeaway>

---

## A smaller bar gets me moving

When I get stuck now, I lower the bar deliberately.

Not "make a beautiful landing page." Make one ugly section with the right information.

Not "write the perfect essay." Write the honest version badly.

Not "build the final feature." Build the smallest prototype that reveals what I do not understand yet.

This is not an excuse to ship careless work. It is a way to separate starting from finishing. Version zero can be ugly because version zero is not the promise. It is the raw material.

The quality comes later, through looking again.

---

If you are holding an idea you have protected for too long, try making it less precious.

Give it twenty minutes. Give it a bad filename. Give it an imperfect first paragraph, a crooked layout, a prototype that only proves the question. Let it be visible enough to disappoint you.

Then tomorrow, make one part better.

A blank page can keep your ego comfortable for a long time. A terrible draft gives your future self something kinder: a place to begin.
