At three in the morning, I was still changing the font on my CV.

Times felt too old. A sans-serif felt too cold. Size 11 looked cramped; 11.5 looked childish. The space between two sections was off by maybe half a millimeter, but at that hour it looked like a public failure. I zoomed the screen to 200%, moved a shadow in the attached portfolio piece one pixel to the right, stared at it, then moved it back.

The next morning I placed that "perfect" version beside the one I had already finished at nine the night before. The difference was almost nothing.

Only I was different: drained, irritated, with dark circles under my eyes and a full day still waiting for me.

---

## "My weakness is that I'm too much of a perfectionist"

I used to be proud of my perfectionism. In interviews, when asked about my weaknesses, I could almost smile and say the classic line: "I can be a bit too much of a perfectionist." It sounded like a flaw wearing a suit.

It took me a long time to admit that the line was not as noble as it sounded.

Healthy standards help you make the work clearer, kinder, more useful. Toxic perfectionism does something different. It keeps polishing after the useful work is done, not because the thing needs it, but because your nervous system still wants insurance.

That night, I was not trying to improve the CV anymore. I was trying to remove every possible reason someone could reject me. No awkward line break. No questionable spacing. No tiny scratch where a stranger could point and say, "See? Not good enough."

Perfection, for me, was not a standard. It was a shield. I held it up because I was afraid that if people looked closely, they would see I was not as capable as I hoped to be.

---

## The cost of the shield

The problem with that shield is that it is heavy, and it charges interest.

First, it takes energy. I poured a whole night into a difference almost nobody could see. That half-millimeter, that one pixel, that slightly more elegant font choice — all of it cost real sleep for an improvement that mostly existed inside my anxiety.

Second, it creates delay. The application email sits in drafts for three days. The post stays unpublished. The portfolio is always "almost ready." You tell yourself you are protecting the quality, but what you are really protecting is the version of you that never has to be judged in public.

> Perfectionism promises to protect you from judgment. In exchange, it quietly takes both your energy and your chance to begin.

I started practicing a phrase that used to make my skin crawl: *done is better than perfect.* Not because sloppy work is admirable, but because finished work can breathe, receive feedback, and become better. The hidden draft can only sit there and feed your fear.

So I began sending the decent eight instead of waiting forever for the imaginary ten. The strange thing was that most of those eights still did their job. Some even got called good. And the energy I saved went into the next application, the next page, the next real attempt.

<KeyTakeaway label="A reminder to myself" tone="amber">
The perfect ten you exhaust yourself chasing is often invisible to other people. A decent eight that ships, learns, and leaves you with strength is usually the braver choice.
</KeyTakeaway>

---

I am not telling you to become careless. Some details matter. Some standards are worth protecting.

I am only asking you to notice when the work has stopped improving and the shield has taken over. You can usually feel the difference in your body. Improvement feels focused. Fear feels frantic.

Before you lose another night to a detail, ask a plain question: *will this change help the person receiving the work, or is it only trying to make me feel unattackable?* If it helps them, keep going. If it only protects your pride, maybe stop.

Lower the shield a little. Let the work be seen while you still have enough energy left to live.
