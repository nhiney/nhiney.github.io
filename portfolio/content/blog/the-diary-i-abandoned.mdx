The notebook slid out from the bottom drawer while I was clearing a pile of old things. Its cover was frayed, the stickers I'd pressed on back then nearly all peeled off, the pages gone yellow like tea left to cool.

I knew it at once: my diary from when I was eighteen. The thing I'd hidden so carefully I forgot I ever had it.

I sat down on the floor and held it for a long while before I dared open it. Honestly, I was afraid. Not of the notebook itself, but of meeting again the girl who had written inside it.

---

## The lines that made me blush

Then I opened it. And just as I'd feared, it was terribly naive.

There were two whole pages about a boy who sat in the row ahead — a careful record of what color shirt he wore that day, how many times he laughed, whether he'd glanced my way by accident. There were grand vows like "I will definitely become the best," written in neat, deliberate handwriting, as if the entire universe were waiting for my announcement. There were worries that make me smile now: failing one test, being left out by a group of friends, wondering whether one awkward sentence had ruined everything.

My first reflex was embarrassment. I even reached to close it, like, "okay, enough, this is too cringe." That eighteen-year-old dreamed too loudly, trusted too quickly, cried over tiny things. I'd traveled so far from her that I almost mistook distance for superiority.

But one line stopped me.

---

## The girl who didn't quit

Near the end of the notebook, I'd written: "So tired today, feel like I've done nothing worthwhile. But fine, I'll get up and try again tomorrow."

I read it again. Then again. The embarrassment slowly dissolved, replaced by something warmer and much harder to swallow.

Because I know what happened after that "try again tomorrow." That tired, clumsy, easily crying eighteen-year-old actually got up. She got up the next day, and the day after, and a thousand days after that. She learned what scared her. She did things with shaking hands. She stood back up after the times she thought she'd gone down for good. She had no proof she'd be okay. She just kept making one ordinary promise: tomorrow, I'll try again.

And that promise carried me here. Sitting on this floor, grown enough to look back at her.

> I used to think my younger self was someone to be embarrassed by. Turns out she was the one quietly carrying me before I knew how to carry myself.

I felt such tenderness for her. The kind you feel for a little sibling giving it everything in a world too big for them. I wanted to climb into that page, sit down beside her, and say: "It's okay. You're doing better than you think. You don't have to be impressive to be worth loving."

<KeyTakeaway label="Something I keep" tone="amber">
Your old self was not a rough draft to be thrown away. She survived with the tools she had, so the version of you today could breathe a little easier.
</KeyTakeaway>

---

I didn't tear that notebook up. I dusted it off and set it back on the shelf — this time somewhere easier to see.

I think we're harsh on our older selves. We look back at who we were three years ago, five years ago, and see only awkwardness, mistakes, naivety. We want to erase the evidence and pretend we were born already wise.

But growth is not proof that the old version was shameful. Growth is proof that she stayed long enough to learn.

So if one day the me of five years from now sits reading the lines I'm writing today, I hope she won't laugh at me. I hope she'll smile, gently, and quietly thank this fumbling girl — for trying, for not quitting, for writing even when she wasn't sure any of it mattered.

Do you have a notebook like that somewhere too? An older version of yourself you still flinch to meet again? If you do, maybe start with this: thank her for getting you this far.
