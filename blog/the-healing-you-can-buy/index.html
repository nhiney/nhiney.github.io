Healing

The Healing You Can Put on a Card

A 'healing' trip, a thing I 'deserved,' and then the next morning the sadness was still there — now with a statement attached. Let me be honest with you about healing.

Nguyễn Thị Yến Nhi

March 12, 2026/4 min read/—views

Eleven at night, I hit "pay" for a trip I hadn't planned at all. Plane ticket, a hotel facing the sea, plus a few things tossed into the cart to "round it out." My finger shook a little, but a sweet voice in my head reassured me: I deserve this. A whole month of dragging myself through work, deadlines breathing down my neck, a few quiet cries. What's wrong with compensating myself a bit.

That swipe gave me a buzz. It lasted about two hours.

The next morning I woke up. And here's the part nobody tells you: the sadness was still lying right there, next to my pillow. It hadn't gone anywhere. It had even invited a new friend to move in — the dread of looking at the number on the statement.

They're selling you your own sadness

Let me say this plainly, because I've fallen for it more than once: there's an entire industry built around our sadness.

Notice it sometime. The moment you're tired, an ad pops up telling you to "treat yourself." The moment you're sad, there's an article suggesting "a healing trip." The moment you feel inadequate, a course, an object, a fancy meal is already waiting, promising to patch the empty spot inside you. People borrow the word "healing" — a beautiful word, one that should be about recovery — and slap it onto anything that can be sold.

And we buy it. I buy a few hours of comfort and call it self-care.

There's a very thin line I keep crossing without noticing: the line between caring for yourself and indulging yourself to the point of self-harm. Self-care is doing the thing tomorrow-me will quietly thank me for. Toxic indulgence is doing the thing that feels good to right-now-me while tomorrow-me pays the price. One mends. The other just slaps a band-aid on and looks away. The trap is this: in the instant you swipe, the two look exactly alike.

Spending money can soothe the ache for a few hours. But it has never once reached the place that's actually hurting.

Real healing is boring

Here's the un-fun truth I wish someone had told me sooner.

Real healing is almost always free. And maddeningly boring.

It's going to bed early, sleeping enough, instead of scrolling until two in the morning scavenging for dopamine. It's drinking enough water, eating a decent meal with something green in it. It's cleaning the room that looks like a battlefield — because often my head is tangled to exactly the degree my room is. It's turning off the sad playlist, putting on shoes, walking two laps outside, calling someone who actually loves me.

And the hardest part, the part no bill can buy for you: sitting still with the bad feeling itself. Not fleeing it on a flight. Not drowning it under a cart. Just sitting there, and asking it one question: what are you actually trying to tell me?

Buying a trip is so much easier than admitting I'm burnt out because I chose the wrong job. Buying a bag is easier than admitting I feel worthless. We spend money to avoid looking at the root — because looking there hurts, and worse, it asks us to change something.

A note to myself

The hole inside you isn't the size of a shopping cart. However many things you pour into it, it won't fill — because it never wanted things in the first place.

I'm not saying spending is a sin. A real trip can genuinely recharge you. A beautiful object can be a perfectly fair joy. The difference fits into one question I ask before I swipe: am I buying this to enjoy it, or am I buying it to hide?

Maturity, for me, turned out to be learning how to soothe myself without wounding my bank account. Knowing the difference between when I truly need rest and when I'm just running away.

Next time your finger is hovering over the "pay" button at midnight, with the familiar excuse "I deserve it" — try pausing for one beat. You do deserve it. But sometimes the thing you deserve most right then isn't in the cart. Sometimes it's just a proper night's sleep, and the courage to wake up tomorrow and look straight at the thing you were about to swipe a card to forget.
