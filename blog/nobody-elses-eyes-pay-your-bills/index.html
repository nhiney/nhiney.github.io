Nobody Else's Eyes Pay Your Bills

A failed deploy, someone else's offer letter, and the reminder that other people's attention cannot do the work your life requires.

Nguyễn Thị Yến Nhi

June 22, 2026/4 min read/—views

At two in the morning, I hit deploy for the fourth time. For the fourth time, the screen stayed red.

The laptop fan was roaring. The coffee beside my hand had gone cold long ago. I watched the logs scroll past without understanding where I had gone wrong. Then, from a very unhelpful habit, I picked up my phone.

A classmate had just posted an offer letter from a big company. Short caption, a few celebration emojis, hundreds of likes. I put the phone down. The red screen was still waiting.

That was all it took for me to feel small enough to want to close the laptop and never open it again.

What that fear is actually called

I used to think this meant I was weak. Later, I learned that the feeling had a name, and that I was far from the only person living with it.

Psychologists call part of it the spotlight effect: the tendency to overestimate how much other people notice us. We imagine a light trained on our every stumble, but most people are busy worrying about their own scene. The bug that made me feel humiliated would be invisible to almost everyone else by morning.

Then comparison adds the second weight. In tech and design, there is always someone to measure against: someone shipping an app over a weekend, someone passing the interview, someone posting a certificate, someone younger and faster and apparently calmer. Meanwhile I am stuck on one error message that refuses to explain itself.

Peer pressure rarely shouts. It whispers: they are already over there. What about you?

A slightly brutal question

Another night, in front of another red screen, I asked myself a blunt question: what do other people's eyes actually do for me?

I do not mean that in a bitter way. Attention can feel good. Approval can make a hard day lighter. But when I looked at my real problems, the answer became clear.

Other people's admiration does not pay my electricity bill. Their judgment does not read the logs with me. Their surprise, praise, or disappointment does not take my exams, send my applications, or build the skill I need next. It all stops at the screen of a phone. It cannot enter the 2 a.m. room where the problem is still mine.

People can watch me all day. But nobody can live a single minute of my life for me.

When that sank in, something became lighter. I did not suddenly stop caring. I still compare. I still feel the sting. But I began asking a more useful question: instead of managing how I look, what can I actually move forward tonight?

What actually changed me

Let me be honest, because otherwise this becomes decorative advice.

What changed me was not one heroic all-nighter. It was the boring repetition afterward: open the laptop, learn one concept, draw one frame, read one dry page of documentation, fix one line, write down what failed. The kind of progress too plain to post about.

The compound interest of effort is frustrating because it is quiet. Today looks the same. A week later, still mostly the same. Then one day you meet a problem that would have crushed you six months ago, and you solve it in ten minutes. The change was happening the whole time, just below the surface.

A note to myself

People rarely get good from the dramatic leap everyone sees. They get good through the evenings no one sees, doing one small useful thing again and again.

The useful part is practical: the more attention I spend on the problem in front of me, the less attention I have left for imaginary audiences. Real work is not a cure for insecurity, but it gives insecurity less room to perform.

I will not end with "just be strong." That sentence does not help much at two in the morning.

What I want to say is simpler: return to the part that belongs to you. One line. One application. One page. One honest attempt.

Let other people have their stages. Your life still has to be lived from your own desk, with your own bills, your own deadlines, your own quiet work. Nobody else's eyes can do that for you.
