Letting Go

The Farmer Who Lost His Horse

A plan fell apart at the last minute, and I wanted to call it failure immediately. The old horse story reminded me to wait before naming the whole thing.

Nguyễn Thị Yến Nhi

January 30, 2026/4 min read/—views

I am very good at building a whole life around a plan that has not happened yet.

Give me one promising possibility and I will furnish the future around it. I will imagine the email, the calendar, the first day, the person I become after everything works out. By the time reality says anything, I may already have moved in.

So when one of those plans collapsed at the last minute, the pain felt larger than the event. A short message. A polite no. A thing outside my control. On paper, not the end of the world.

In my head, an entire imagined house lost its roof.

I sat there with the hollow feeling first, then anger, then that childish but very human question: why now, when I had already pictured it so clearly?

The hidden grief of losing control

I thought I was grieving the opportunity.

Partly, yes. But I was also grieving the illusion that life had agreed to my script. I had done the planning, the effort, the visualizing. Some part of me believed reality should now cooperate.

That belief makes disappointment sharper. When life swerves, we do not only lose the thing. We lose the comforting story that if we are careful enough, nothing can surprise us.

In that mood, I remembered the old story of the farmer whose horse ran away. The neighbors came to pity him, and he said, "Maybe." The horse returned with another horse, and the neighbors celebrated. "Maybe," he said again. His son rode the new horse, fell, and broke his leg. Disaster, everyone thought. Then soldiers came to take young men to war, and the son stayed home because of the injury.

The point is not that every bad thing is secretly good. That would be too neat, and life is not that tidy.

The point is that the farmer did not rush to name the whole story while standing inside one unfinished scene.

I am quick to call something ruin when I may only be looking at a turn in the road.

Not every detour is a punishment

With distance, some old disappointments have changed shape.

The project I wanted badly left an empty season when it failed, and that empty season gave me time to learn a different skill. The person I thought I had lost left space for relationships that felt less like proving and more like breathing. The missed train, the rejected application, the plan that slipped through my fingers: each one felt like evidence that life was working against me. Later, some became evidence that life was larger than my first draft.

I want to be careful here. Not every pain needs to be justified. Some things are simply hard. Some losses remain losses. We do not have to decorate grief to make it acceptable.

But it helps me to remember that my first interpretation is not always the final truth.

At the moment a plan breaks, I rarely have enough information. I see one piece and judge the entire picture. I call it failure before I know what the space might make possible. I call it delay before I know what I am being slowed down for.

A note I keep

When a plan breaks, I can grieve it without immediately deciding what it means. Not every detour is a verdict. Some are just the road changing shape.

Let the next road reveal itself

Now, when something falls apart, I do not pretend to be calm right away.

I am disappointed. I complain. I replay the details. I let myself have a day or two of being very human about it. Acceptance that skips grief is usually just performance in softer clothes.

Then, after the first wave, I try to loosen my grip on the label.

Maybe this is bad. Maybe it is protection. Maybe it is a delay. Maybe it is a redirection. Maybe it is simply something I cannot understand yet.

That small "maybe" does not fix the loss. It gives the future a little room to breathe.

If a plan has just broken in your hands, you do not have to call it beautiful. You do not have to be grateful on command. Just try, if you can, not to close the case too early.

Let yourself hurt. Then keep your eyes open.

The road may not be obeying your map. That does not mean there is no road.
