Self Worth

You Don't Have to Be the Sun

A small ache after reading about someone else's spectacular success, and a quieter way to measure a life that still matters.

Nguyễn Thị Yến Nhi

June 26, 2026/4 min read/—views

Last night I was lying in bed scrolling my phone when I found an article about someone born in the late nineties who had just landed on a prestigious ranking. They had raised a few million dollars, built something people called "industry-changing," and stood in the photo under bright stage lights while a room full of people clapped.

I felt the sting before I had time to pretend I was above it.

It lasted maybe three seconds. Then I put the phone down, reached for the bowl of soup still warm on my desk, and took a sip. It was hot, a little salty, exactly what I needed. The ache dissolved, but one question stayed behind: what if I never do anything big with my whole life?

The quiet pressure to be exceptional

I think many of us grow up with a belief installed so early that we mistake it for truth.

Movies are about chosen ones. Fairy tales are about ordinary children who turn out to carry a hidden destiny. Even school prizes, social media bios, and graduation speeches keep telling us to stand out, leave a mark, become someone worth remembering.

That message sounds inspiring until it becomes the only way you know how to value yourself.

Once you believe you must be extraordinary, ordinary good things begin to look like failure. A stable job that lets you pay rent: not enough. A clean handoff to a teammate: not enough. Dinner cooked after a tiring day: not enough. Somehow the scale has only one mark, placed absurdly high: be impressive, or be invisible.

I strained toward that mark for a long time. It made every normal day feel like evidence against me. Even when nobody was asking me to save the world, I kept living as if my worth was still waiting for one grand announcement.

This world runs on ordinary people

Another evening, I sat thinking it over, and one very plain idea made my whole body lighter.

The world does not run because a few great names appear on rankings. It runs because billions of ordinary people do their small parts well enough, often without applause.

The bus arrived this morning because a driver no one can name woke before sunrise. The bowl of phở tasted right because someone watched the broth for hours. The app opened smoothly because a developer fixed a boring bug that will never become a launch story. None of them "reinvented an industry." Remove enough of them, though, and daily life begins to fall apart.

The whole world looks up at the sun. But the person who finds their way home at night does it by a streetlamp nobody notices.

When I look at it that way, my own quiet work changes shape. Designing an interface that makes one task less confusing. Fixing a bug before it ruins someone's morning. Answering a message with patience. Supporting myself without making my life look dramatic online. These things are not small. They are just not loud.

Something I keep

Ordinary is not a failed version of extraordinary. It is the floor life stands on. Doing one small thing well, steadily, and kindly is already a meaningful way to be here.

Some light is meant to be local

So I am trying to stop forcing myself to burn.

I let myself respect the small things I actually live: finishing a task and exhaling, eating noodles with a fried egg when I am hungry, rewatching an old film because it still comforts me. There is no dramatic ambition in any of that. But there is a life.

If my life never blazes like the sun, I can still be a streetlamp. One corner. A small circle of light. Enough for a few people to see the next step when the road is dark. No applause, no photos, but present at the exact scale where I can actually be useful.

I am not writing this to make ambition sound foolish. If you want to build something large, go build it with care. I only want to leave a note for the nights when someone else's spotlight makes your own room feel dim: you do not have to become remarkable before you are allowed to feel at peace.

A life where you sleep without dread, eat with appetite, keep promises to a few people, and do honest work may not trend anywhere. It is still not a consolation prize.

The soup has gone cold now. I am going to reheat it, and for tonight, that is enough.
