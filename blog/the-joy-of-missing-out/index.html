The Joy of Being a Little Out of the Loop

One morning I opened my phone and the whole world was arguing about a drama I knew nothing about. And for the first time, I felt relieved to have missed it.

Nguyễn Thị Yến Nhi

June 26, 2026/4 min read/—views

This morning I opened my phone and immediately felt lost. The whole feed was boiling over something — a celebrity had done something, a quote chopped out of context, a trend everyone was filming themselves copying. People arguing, throwing angry reactions, writing whole essays to declare where they stood. And there I was, blinking like I'd just gotten back from somewhere far away, with no clue what any of it was about.

My first reflex, honestly, was a small panic. Like: wait, did I just miss something? My thumb was already drifting in to read it all, to "get up to speed," to not be the clueless one. But then I caught myself. I turned the phone face-down on the table. And a strange feeling came over me. Not regret. Relief.

The fear we call FOMO

I grew up with a fear I didn't even have a name for back then. Later people gave it three letters: FOMO — the fear of missing out.

It's quiet. It's the reason I unlocked my phone a hundred times a day with nothing waiting there. It's the restlessness of seeing friends out somewhere without me. It's forcing myself to know the hot song, the show everyone's watching, the new café everyone's checking into — as if falling one beat behind meant the whole world would leave me there.

I think this era is built to feed exactly that fear. Every app on my phone wants me to stay a little longer, swipe one more time, dread missing one more thing. And I obeyed, like a good kid. I was "online" almost always, not because it made me happy, but because I was scared. Scared of being absent. Scared of being forgotten. Scared of being the only one who didn't know.

It's tiring. Genuinely tiring. The kind of tired no amount of sleep fixes, because it doesn't live in your body. It lives in a head that's always on watch.

Turns out I didn't miss a thing

That day I switched everything off for 24 hours, I thought I'd be itchy and miserable. But by noon something settled. By evening I could hear the rain against the window — a sound I probably hadn't truly heard in a year, because my ears were always busy with a screen.

And the next morning, while the internet was still in an uproar, I noticed something almost funny: the drama everyone treated like the end of the world, nobody remembered three days later. I "missed" it, and my life didn't shift a single inch.

It turns out most of what I'm afraid to miss is exactly what I don't need in order to live.

People say luxury now is designer bags, fancy trips. To me the real luxury is quiet. The right to not know. To not have an opinion about everything under the sun. To be absent from one more good time without feeling guilty about it.

What I'm keeping

I don't have to be everywhere to prove I'm alive. Sometimes I'm most alive when I switch it all off, and it's just me and my own afternoon.

I started practicing something I jokingly call "the art of being a little behind the times." Don't know the newest trend? Fine. Haven't seen the show everyone's seen? It'll keep. Someone mentions a meme I don't get? I smile, nod, and let it go. The need to know everything slid off my shoulders without my noticing. Unbelievably light.

I'm not telling you to smash your phone or move to a mountain. The internet has its good sides, and I still use it every day. I just want to invite you to try it once: let the world out there keep being loud, and take one step back from it.

Close the door. Mute the notifications. Make yourself a cup of tea and sit there while it cools in your hands. The world keeps turning just fine without you tracking it second by second. And you know what — being allowed to miss out, that's where the freedom is.
