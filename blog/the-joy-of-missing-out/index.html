The Joy of Being a Little Out of the Loop

One morning I opened my phone and found the whole internet arguing about something I knew nothing about. For once, missing it felt less like failure and more like freedom.

Nguyễn Thị Yến Nhi

June 26, 2026/5 min read/—views

This morning I opened my phone and immediately felt behind.

The whole feed was boiling over something. A celebrity had said something. Someone had cut a quote out of context. A trend had appeared overnight, and apparently everyone had already filmed their version of it. People were arguing, posting reactions, writing long paragraphs to announce exactly where they stood.

And there I was, staring at the screen like someone who had just returned from a very remote mountain.

My first reflex was panic. Not big panic, just the small modern kind. Wait, did I miss something? Should I know this? Will I sound clueless if someone brings it up later?

My thumb moved toward the thread. I was about to catch up, not because I cared, but because I did not want to be the only person who did not know.

Then I stopped. I put the phone face-down on the table.

And the feeling that arrived was not regret.

It was relief.

FOMO does not always look dramatic

I grew up with a fear I did not have a neat name for at first. Later the internet gave it one: FOMO, the fear of missing out.

People talk about FOMO like it only means wanting to attend parties. But mine was quieter than that.

It was unlocking my phone a hundred times a day with nothing specific waiting for me. It was feeling oddly wounded when friends went somewhere without me, even when I was too tired to go. It was forcing myself to know the hot song, the show everyone was watching, the restaurant everyone was checking into, the argument everyone had an opinion about.

As if falling one beat behind meant the world would move on and forget to leave a door open.

This era is designed to feed exactly that fear. Every app wants me to stay a little longer, swipe one more time, watch one more clip, refresh one more page. The message is never spoken directly, but it is there: if you leave, something might happen without you.

So I stayed. Not always because it made me happy. Often because I was scared of being absent.

That kind of attention is expensive. It spends the mind in tiny coins all day until, by evening, you cannot understand why you feel poor.

Most things did not need me in the room

The day I turned things off for 24 hours, I expected to feel restless. For the first few hours, I did. My hand kept reaching for my phone like it had its own little memory.

By noon, the reaching slowed down.

By evening, I heard rain against the window. Not as background noise under a video, not as something I half-noticed while scrolling, but as an actual sound in the room with me. I made tea and forgot it long enough that it cooled in my hands. I read three pages of a book without checking anything. I stared at the wall, which sounds boring until you realize how rare it is to let your mind be unoccupied without immediately renting it out to a screen.

The next morning, the internet was still loud. But something funny had happened: the drama that seemed urgent the day before had already started dissolving. Three days later, almost nobody cared.

I had missed it, and my life had not shifted an inch.

Most of what I am afraid to miss is not actually required for the life I want to live.

That sentence changed the texture of my days.

People often talk about luxury as expensive things: nice hotels, designer bags, beautiful trips. I understand that. But more and more, the luxury I crave is quieter. The right not to know. The right not to have an opinion ready. The right to be absent from one more conversation without turning that absence into a personality flaw.

What I'm keeping

I do not have to be everywhere to prove I am alive. Sometimes I feel most alive when I let the world continue without my supervision.

I started practicing what I jokingly call the art of being a little behind.

Do I know the newest trend? Not always. Have I watched the show everyone is discussing? Maybe later. Did I miss a group hangout because I needed a quiet evening? Yes, and the friendship survived. Does someone mention a meme I do not understand? I can smile and ask, or I can let it pass.

The surprising part is how little collapses.

The group chat continues. The internet finds a new emergency. The world remains fully committed to spinning without my constant attendance.

And in the space that opens, I get small pieces of myself back.

I am not telling you to smash your phone or move to a mountain. The internet is useful. It brings friendship, work, beauty, absurd jokes, and ideas I would never find alone. I still use it every day.

But I am learning to stop treating every new thing as a summons.

Not every notification is an invitation. Not every debate needs my nervous little attendance. Not every fun event becomes a judgment on my life if I choose to stay home.

If you feel tired in a way sleep does not fix, try missing something on purpose.

Mute one thread. Skip one drama. Let one trend pass without studying it. Spend an afternoon slightly unreachable. Make tea. Walk without documenting it. Sit in a quiet room long enough for your own thoughts to stop feeling like strangers.

The world will keep moving.

That used to scare me.

Now, sometimes, it comforts me.
