Today I Wasted a Whole Afternoon — and Needed It

Not every hour has to become output. Some afternoons return you to yourself.

Nguyễn Thị Yến Nhi

June 29, 2026/4 min read/—views

I have a confession.

Today I spent three hours doing nothing useful.

Actually, that is not quite true. I did several things. I watched clouds move slowly across the sky. I drew a round cat in the corner of my notebook, though it looked more like a dumpling with ears. I walked to the end of the alley, bought a milk tea flavor I still cannot identify, sat on a front step, and watched people pass by.

No deadline. No Figma. No tutorial. No "next step in the roadmap."

And I felt wonderfully human.

Why does rest need a defense?

I am not sure when doing nothing became something we had to explain.

Ask someone what they did over the weekend and the safest answer is usually productive: finished a course, cleaned the room, built a side project, worked on a portfolio, learned a new tool. Say "I let my mind wander for an afternoon" and people may smile like you have just admitted to wasting food.

We are told that time is limited, youth is capital, and someone else is always working harder. There is truth in that. Discipline matters. Effort matters. I do not want a life made only of soft excuses.

But a life made only of optimized hours becomes strangely thin.

The most "useless" moments often give creativity the space it needs to breathe. The afternoon I spent watching clouds did not teach me a measurable skill, but later that evening I finally solved a layout problem that had been stuck in my head for days. The ridiculous cat in my notebook served no project, but after drawing it my thoughts felt less tangled. The aimless walk produced no output, but it returned a feeling I lose when every minute is assigned a job: the feeling that I am living, not merely processing tasks.

Good ideas rarely arrive because we order them to. They appear in the shower, on a walk, while waiting for water to boil, while looking out a window for longer than seems reasonable. The mind needs idle space to connect what our schedules keep separating.

Sometimes "wasting time" is simply rest doing its work in a form we do not know how to measure.

Memories are not a side project

I sometimes ask myself a simple question: when I look back at my twenties, what do I want to remember?

I want achievements, of course. I want proof that I tried, learned, built, and became more capable.

But I also want the afternoon on the front step with a strange milk tea. The walk that accidentally introduced me to a bakery I now recognize by smell. The evening I bought a children's coloring book because it made me laugh, then spent an hour filling it in with no purpose at all.

Those memories will never look impressive on paper. They are still part of the life I am trying so hard to build.

Money can be earned again. A missed online course can be watched later. A portfolio can be improved next month. But the openness, health, silliness, and freedom of a particular season of life do not repeat in exactly the same shape.

I do not want to arrive at thirty with a clean productivity record and no texture in the years behind me.

The quiet, "unproductive" moments are not empty space. They are the rests in a piece of music. Without the rests, there is no rhythm. Without rhythm, even effort becomes noise.

A small assignment in being alive

I know you may be reading this beside a to-do list.

I am not going to tell you to abandon your responsibilities. That would be lazy advice. I am only suggesting one small experiment.

Pick one afternoon this week. Close the laptop for a while. Go outside without turning the walk into content, exercise data, or a productivity ritual. Buy something you like to drink. Walk slowly. Notice the balcony plants, the old signboard, the person sweeping in front of a shop, the color of the sky right before evening.

Do not take a photo just to prove it happened. Do not turn on a podcast to improve yourself in the background.

Just be there.

That afternoon may not move your career forward in any visible way. But it might return something more basic: the sense that your life is not waiting somewhere after all the work is done.

Your life is already here.

Do not spend all of it preparing to begin.
