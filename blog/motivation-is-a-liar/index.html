Motivation Is a Liar

Motivation is too moody to steer a real life. This is a note for the mornings when your brain gives you excellent reasons to skip, and for the small discipline that gets you moving anyway.

Nguyễn Thị Yến Nhi

June 26, 2026/5 min read/—views

This morning I woke up before my alarm, and the first thing my brain did was not get up. It opened negotiations.

The sky was grey in that heavy way that makes staying in bed feel almost philosophical. My head ached a little. I had gone to sleep too late. Yesterday had been long. Skipping one morning would not ruin my life. The blanket was warm, persuasive, and honestly making some excellent points.

In about ten seconds, my brain had prepared a full presentation on why I should rest "just this once."

I lay there listening to the argument. One voice whispered, "You're not feeling great. Make up for it tomorrow." Another voice, much less polite, asked, "How many times have you said that?"

That was the moment a slightly brutal truth landed: what I had been calling "waiting for motivation" was often just me waiting for an emotion to come and do my job for me.

Motivation is weather, not fuel

I used to think motivation was fuel. Wait for the tank to fill, then start the engine.

That sounds reasonable until you notice how rarely the tank fills on schedule.

Motivation is not fuel. It is weather. Sunny one morning, heavy by noon, gone by the time you finally sit down to work. It rises and falls with sleep, stress, blood sugar, the last message you read, the tiny disappointment you did not admit bothered you.

And yet I had placed very serious promises on top of that unstable ground.

Waiting for motivation to build a disciplined life is like handing the steering wheel to someone who only drives well when the mood is right. On good days, he speeds down the road and I think, this is it, I'm finally becoming that person. Then one curve appears, his mood changes, and suddenly I am in the back seat asking why we are in a ditch.

There is another uncomfortable piece: the brain is not designed to make my dreams come true. It is designed to save energy and avoid pain. Anything hard gets flagged as expensive. Studying, writing, exercising, thinking deeply, starting the scary conversation. Anything easy with a quick reward gets waved through. Scroll. Snack. Watch one more video. Answer a message that feels productive but is not the work.

That does not mean I am broken. It means my brain is doing an old job in a world full of modern shortcuts.

The problem is that a comfortable brain does not automatically create a meaningful life.

Stop asking whether you feel like it

My turning point began when I stopped treating discipline as self-punishment.

Discipline is not a dramatic war against yourself. At its best, it is an agreement made while you are clear-headed, so that the tired version of you does not get to renegotiate everything from scratch.

The practical change was small: I stopped asking, "Do I want to do this?"

That question gives my brain too much room. Do I want to write when the first paragraph is bad? Usually no. Do I want to exercise when the floor is cold? No. Do I want to do the unglamorous admin task that will save me trouble next week? Absolutely not.

So I changed the question to: "Does this need doing?"

If the answer is yes, the next step has to be smaller than my resistance. Open the laptop. Put on the shoes. Write one ugly paragraph. Clean one corner. Send one message. Not become a new person by sunrise. Just begin.

What I learned

Action creates inspiration more reliably than inspiration creates action. Do the first ugly five minutes, and the feeling often catches up later.

I have tested this enough times to trust it. On sluggish days, I give myself one plain instruction: start badly, but start. Ten minutes in, the work usually becomes less dramatic. My head cools down. The task stops looking like a mountain and starts looking like a series of steps.

The motivation I waited for all morning often arrives only after I have already begun. Like someone who refuses to join until the game is clearly happening.

That is why motivation feels like a liar to me. Not because the feeling is fake, but because it promises to lead and then often shows up late. Useful when it comes. Dangerous when it is in charge.

If you are under the blanket this morning too, listening to your mind read out a very reasonable list of reasons to skip, I will not pretend the reasons are all nonsense. Maybe you are tired. Maybe the week has been heavy. Maybe rest is genuinely what you need.

But if you know, quietly, that this is not rest and only avoidance wearing nicer clothes, try making the first step small enough that your excuses look overdressed.

You do not have to feel heroic.

Put your feet on the floor. Drink water. Open the file. Do five minutes. Let it be unimpressive.

The people who finish things are not always burning with energy. Most of them are just less willing to let a passing mood vote on every promise they made to their future self.

The hardest moment is often not the work. It is the tiny transfer of authority from "I don't feel like it" to "this still matters."

Do that once today. Small, plain, uncinematic.

That counts.
